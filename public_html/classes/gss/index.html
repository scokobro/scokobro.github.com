    Global Studies Seminar: News

    Centred around weekly readings of classic academic work on news and journalism this course introduces students to a broad range of material and provides a basis for further study of newsmaking.

    Deadlines for Review Submission

    The assessment for this course requires you to submit a review of one of the chapters in the course book. This should be 2000 words long and aim to satisfy the type of requirements outlined in the rubric (see link below).

    Final Submissions: 19 January
    Please submit by email; the subject line should be ‘GSS Final: [your name]’. Please send either MSWord (‘doc’ or ‘docx’) or OpenDocument (‘odt’) files, don’t send me PDFs or Mac Pages documents unless it’s completely unavoidable. Thanks.

    Syllabus

    Course Outline and Method

    A discussion of the idea of ‘news’ - what it is, where it comes from, why it’s like it is etc - based on the selections in Howard TUMBER (1999). “News: A Reader”, with some related additions to bring it more up to date. This class is centred on student presentation of the themed weekly reading material and discussion of the issues raised.

    Student Attainment Objectives

    1. Be aware of various definitions of ‘news’.
    2. Be able to explain the differences between and evaluate these various approaches.
    3. Be aware of the link between various actors in the production of news and how they each influence the final content.
    4. Improved ability to read and critique academic writing.

    Session Schedule

    1. Introductions
      Explain the aims of the course. Check syllabus details and assign first set of presenters for week 2.
    2 – 3. What is News?
    How do we define news? How do those in the news industries define it? How do these definitions affect the content and form of news?
    4 – 6. How is news produced?
    How are various types of news produced? Who is involved and in what roles? Why do news organisations work the way they do?
    7 – 8. Economics of news
    Who owns what? Why does this matter? How do the business structures of news organisations affect news content?
    9 – 10. Sources of News
    Who’s in the news and why? Why is ‘diversity’ important? Who gets to define what an news event ‘means’?
    11 – 14. Objectivity and Ideology
    Objectivity (neutrality, fairness etc), professionalism and the network of ideas that underpin how creators of news understand their work. How these ideas form our expectations of news content.
    15. Summary and Review
    Review main points of course and final feedback.

    Assessment

    Class participation: 40%

    Prepare for class by reading the set material, and appropriate questions. Presenters (and back-up presenters) are expected to be ready to explain the content of the reading to class members in a brief (10-15mins), informal manner.

    Active efforts to take part in discussion, willingness to express ideas and ask appropriate questions. During the course it is your responsibility to provide me with evidence that you are attempting to engage with the material in a thoughtful and critical manner, you can do this primarily by asking questions and joining in with discussions in class.

    NB. If you are absent five times or more without due reason you will automatically be awarded an F grade.

    Paper Review: 60%

    This will be a ‘review’ of (where possible) a FULL version of material excerpted in Tumber, or elsewhere in the course material.

    Final Deadline: week 15 Word count: 2000

    A rubric (assessment guidelines) will be distributed to give you an idea of what you should be aiming to achieve with this piece of work, and how I will assess it and award a grade. You will be asked to submit a draft version of your review roughly half-way through the semester and one session will be put aside for feedback on theses drafts.

    Please be aware that I use the Turnitin software to check all written submissions. If you have objections to your work being submitted for plagiarism checking, please discuss it with me. Plagiarism will not be tolerated. You must ensure that you are aware of what constitutes plagiarism and of the university’s policies regarding plagiarised work. YOU are responsible for your participation in this course, I will not remind you to attend class and I will not remind to hand in work on time.

    References

    Tumber, H. (1999). News: A Reader. Oxford University Press, Oxford.

    NB. I have several copies of this book which are available for loan on a first come first served basis. A deposit of 2000yen is required if you wish to borrow a copy.
